II. Time Series Fundamentals (Slides 4-7)

Slide 4: Decomposition: The Four Components

Time series data \(Y_t\) is structured by combining these four elements:

  1. Trend (\(T_t\)): Long-term, smooth movement (e.g., general market growth).
  2. Seasonality (\(S_t\)): Repeating patterns at fixed intervals (e.g., quarterly spikes).
  3. Cyclic Patterns: Patterns without fixed intervals (e.g., economic cycles).
  4. Irregularity (\(\epsilon_t\)): Random noise.
  • Additive Model: \[Y_t = S_t + T_t + \epsilon_t\]
  • Multiplicative Model: \[Y_t = S_t \times T_t \times \epsilon_t\]

Slide 5: Autocorrelation (ACF)

  • What is it? Measures the linear relationship between observations at different time lags.
  • Purpose: Essential for identifying the structure of a time series model (e.g., setting the p and q parameters in ARIMA).

\[\hat{\rho}_k = \frac{\sum_{t=k+1}^{T} (y_t - \bar{y})(y_{t-k} - \bar{y})}{\sum_{t=1}^{T} (y_t - \bar{y})^2}\]

Slide 6: Stationarity

  • Definition: A stationary series has statistical properties (mean, variance, autocorrelation) that are constant over time.
  • Why it Matters: Many powerful time series models (like ARIMA) require the data to be stationary.
  • Non-Stationary Stock Data: Stock prices are typically non-stationary (they tend to trend upward), requiring a differencing step.

III. The ARIMA Model (Slides 8-11)

Slide 8: Introducing ARIMA

AutoRegressive Integrated Moving Average

ARIMA models explain a time series based on its lags and lagged forecast errors.

The model is defined by three parameters: \[(p, d, q)\]

  • p: AutoRegressive (AR) Order (Lags of the dependent variable)
  • d: Integrated (I) Order (Number of differences for stationarity)
  • q: Moving Average (MA) Order (Lags of the forecast errors)

Slide 11: Cross-Validation & Error Metrics

To trust the forecast, we must validate the model against held-out test data (last 60 days).

  • MAE (Mean Absolute Error): Average forecast deviation. \[MAE = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n}|y_t - \hat{y}_t|\]
  • RMSE (Root Mean Square Error): Penalizes large errors more heavily. \[RMSE = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n}(y_t - \hat{y}_t)^2}\]
  • MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error): Error as a percentage of the actual value. \[MAPE = \frac{100\%}{n}\sum_{t=1}^{n}\left|\frac{y_t - \hat{y}_t}{y_t}\right|\]

Slide 13: The Investment Loop

To find the best stock, we apply the automated ARIMA fitting and forecasting to all 30 stocks based on monthly averaged data.

  • Steps for each Ticker:
    1. Aggregate daily data to monthly mean.
    2. Split monthly data for Train/Test (last 3 months for testing).
    3. Calculate RMSE and MAPE on test set (Model Reliability).
    4. Fit final ARIMA model on all data.
    5. Forecast 60 steps ahead (1 month).
    6. Calculate Predicted Change = Forecast End Price - Last Observed Price.

Slide 18: Areas for Model Improvement

Going Beyond Simple ARIMA

  1. Exogenous Variables (ARIMAX): Incorporate external factors like interest rates or consumer confidence.
  2. Advanced Models:
    • GARCH: To model and forecast volatility.
    • Neural Networks (LSTM): To capture complex, non-linear dependencies.
  3. Data Preparation: Further cleansing, normalization, and handling of outliers.

Slide 19: Higher-Level Trading Context

  • Language Choice: For actual high-frequency trading, lower-level, optimized languages like Ocaml, Rust, or Python with optimized libraries are preferred over R.
  • Wider Scope: This project only scratches the surface; the entire field relies heavily on stochastic calculus and advanced probability theory.
